Abstract

Informal firms possess invisible capital—location rents, client networks, accumulated know-how—that never appears on a balance sheet and cannot be recovered from a household survey. Using a seven-day double-entry financial diary administered to 500 informal firms across Bolivia (25,338 transaction records), we construct complete income statements, balance sheets, and going-concern valuations.

The central finding: 43 percent of informal firms are loss-making after imputing owner and family labor at minimum wage and depreciation at sector-specific rates. The median accounting gap—Bs 1,002/week—is driven 88% by imputed labor. These loss-making firms persist because operating cash flow remains positive: 98% generate cash weekly, masking the accounting loss.
