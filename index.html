Research Program

Bolivia has the highest informality rate in Latin America—85% of workers, 62% of GDP. This research program uses a purpose-built financial diary administered to 500 informal firms across six sectors and four cities (La Paz/El Alto, Cochabamba, Santa Cruz, Tarija) to understand why loss-making firms persist.

Central finding: 43% of informal firms are loss-making after imputing owner labor at shadow wage—yet 97% have positive cash flow. The "cash flow veil" explains persistence: owners observe cash, not economic profit. Among loss-makers, 70% would decline a formal job offer because their cash flow exceeds the formal wage. Active labor market programs targeting informal workers are mistargeted 2.96:1.

Papers

Ledgers of the Self-Employed: Accounting for the Invisible Firm
Working Paper · Measurement Companion

Using a seven-day double-entry financial diary, we construct complete income statements, balance sheets, and going-concern valuations for 500 informal firms. The median accounting gap—Bs 1,002/week—is driven 88% by imputed labor. Median Tobin's Q for viable firms is 5.7, implying going-concern values far exceed liquidation values. True debt prevalence is 49% vs. 7% detected by household surveys.

Trapped by Cash: A Theory of Voluntary Informality Among the Economically Non-Viable
Working Paper · Theory + Empirics

Why do loss-making informal firms persist? Standard models assume rationing: formal jobs are scarce. This paper proposes a different mechanism for 70% of loss-makers: the cash flow veil. Owners observe cash flow, not economic profit. If cash flow exceeds the formal wage, they decline formal offers—even while losing money. I develop a model with dual embedded capital (productive vs. relational) and show it generates a mass of persistent loss-makers in equilibrium. ALMP mistargeting ratio: 2.96:1.
