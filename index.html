§2

The Profit Reversal

The central finding. Every firm in the sample appears profitable under the conventional accounting implied by Bolivia's household survey. Once we impose proper double-entry accounting — crediting owner and family labour at the minimum wage, charging depreciation on productive assets, and adding informal interest payments hidden from survey enumerators — 57% of firms become loss-making. The median accounting gap is Bs 1,191/week, driven 88% by unpaid labour. This is not a modelling choice: it follows directly from hours worked (median 55 hrs/week) valued at Bs 13.64/hr. The waterfall chart below shows exactly how the gap is constructed — revenue, subtract each cost layer, arrive at conventional profit, then subtract the three invisible costs to reach the adjusted truth.

III — Debt-trapped (profit > 0, high debt)

§4

Why Loss-Making Firms Keep Operating

The puzzle and its answer. If 57% of firms have negative adjusted income, why don't they close? The answer is not irrationality — it is six distinct mechanisms visible in the diary. The most fundamental is shown in the first chart: 100% of loss-making firms have positive operating cash flow (median Bs 807/week). The accounting loss is entirely non-cash — imputed labour never leaves the till, depreciation is never invoiced. The firm feels viable from the inside. The second chart shows the hourly return reality: most workers earn below the minimum wage, but the outside option (formal employment) largely does not exist in Bolivia's labour market, so quitting means zero income, not a better job. The third chart shows the household liquidity function: all 300 firms make daily cash draws for household consumption — the firm is the family's bank account, and closing it destroys the household's primary liquidity mechanism.
Mechanism 1: cash flow vs adjusted profit — loss-making firms only
Every dot is above the red zero line — all 171 loss-making firms have positive operating cash flow. The accounting loss exists only on paper, not in the cash register. This is why owners do not perceive their own losses.
Mechanism 2: hourly adjusted return vs minimum wage (Bs/hr)
Red = earning a negative return per hour worked. Amber = earning something, but below the legal minimum wage. Green = above. The vertical line marks Bs 13.64/hr — Bolivia's statutory minimum. Most workers fall in the red or amber zone.
Mechanism 3: household extraction rate (% of weekly revenue withdrawn)
All 300 firms make daily withdrawals from the firm's cash for household use (median 10.1% of weekly revenue). The informal firm is the household's current account, savings account, and emergency fund simultaneously. Closing it destroys this.
§5

Survey vs Diary — The Measurement Gap

What the survey misses. Bolivia's Encuesta de Hogares asks the self-employed to recall annual revenue and eight cost categories. The diary records every transaction for seven consecutive days with double-entry discipline. The comparison reveals a compound measurement error of ambiguous sign: surveys omit costs (which inflates measured profit) but also under-record revenue (which deflates it), and the net direction varies by sector. This means you cannot fix the bias by applying a single correction factor — the error is structural. The scorecard result: 13 of 24 financial concepts needed to compute true profit are entirely absent from the survey instrument, including all four that determine whether a firm is above or below the loss threshold.
EH survey vs diary-adjusted annual net income — one dot per firm (Bs/year)
Points on the 45° line: survey and diary agree. Points below the line: survey overstates net income (cost omission dominates). Points above: diary detects more income than the survey (revenue under-declaration dominates). The majority of points fall below the line.
Diary-to-EH income ratio by sector (1.0 = perfect agreement)
Green bar = diary income exceeds survey income (survey under-counts). Red bar = survey income exceeds diary income (survey overcounts due to cost omission). The direction differs by sector — proving the bias is not a simple scalar and cannot be fixed with a uniform adjustment.
§6

Financial Ratios & Going-Concern Value

Corporate finance applied to informal firms. For the first time, the full battery of financial ratios used in corporate analysis is computed from transaction-level data for informal enterprises. Return on Assets (ROA) collapses dramatically once labour is properly accounted for. The most striking result is Tobin's Q for the viable minority: the going-concern present value (discounted at the informal-sector rate of 18%) exceeds book value of physical assets by a median factor of 83×. This invisible capital — the market stall location, loyal customers, operational knowledge — is real. It is what formalisation policy promises to unlock. The debt chart reveals a two-tier credit market: regulated lenders (IFD/cooperatives) at ~25% EAR, and informal moneylenders (prestamistas) at a median of 201% EAR — which Bolivia's household survey fails to detect in 97% of cases.
Return on assets — conventional (survey) vs adjusted (diary), annualised %
Blue = ROA as the survey implies (always positive, often very high). Red = true adjusted ROA from the diary. The adjustment shifts the distribution sharply leftward — most firms move from apparently high returns to zero or negative territory.
Tobin's Q — going-concern present value ÷ book assets (viable firms only)
Q = 1 means the firm is worth exactly its physical assets. Q = 83 (median) means the firm's location, clients, and knowledge are worth 82× more than its tools and inventory. This invisible capital cannot be collateralised or transferred without formal title.
Effective annual interest rate (EAR) by lender type
Box = interquartile range, line = median, dot = mean. IFD/cooperatives lend at ~25% EAR. Informal moneylenders charge a median of 201% EAR. Bolivia's Encuesta de Hogares detects only 3% of true debt — a detection rate of 7 in 100 cases.
§7

Tax Policy Simulator — IVA Incidence

What the slider does. Bolivia's IVA (value-added tax / sales tax) is currently 13%. The slider lets you explore what happens at any rate from 0% to 25%. The IVA is applied to gross revenue, which means it is levied regardless of whether the firm is actually profitable. For a Viable firm, it reduces profit but may not cause a loss. For a Precaria firm already earning negative adjusted income, the IVA further deepens the loss — the tax falls on a worker who is already earning below the minimum wage. Note the baseline at 0%: even with no tax at all, 57% of firms are already loss-making. The IVA does not create the disguised unemployment problem — but it intensifies it.
